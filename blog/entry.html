STREAMING DEV LIFE

LEVEL 01: WHY I STARTED STREAMING CODE

It started as an accident. I was pair programming with a friend over Discord, sharing my screen, and someone else joined the call just to watch. They stayed for two hours. At some point they said "this is better than anything on TV right now" and I didn't know what to do with that.

A week later I set up OBS, hit the "Go Live" button on a whim, and started building a feature in an open source education project. Thirteen people showed up. One of them caught a bug I'd been staring at for forty minutes. We fixed it in four minutes together.

THE THING NOBODY TELLS YOU

Streaming code is uncomfortable in the exact right way. You can't hide your process. Every confused look at the docs, every backspace, every console.log("HERE") — it's all out there. That vulnerability turns out to be the interesting part.

Programming is usually a solitary, opaque activity. You hand people a finished thing and they have no idea how it was built. Streaming inverts that. The process becomes the product.

Watching someone figure something out in real time is more compelling than watching someone demonstrate mastery. Mastery is impressive. Figuring things out is relatable.

WHAT I STREAM

Two categories. First: actual work on real projects — education software, side projects, open source contributions. Real codebases, real constraints, real blockers.

Second: games. Mostly rhythm games and precision platformers. BIT.TRIP. Celeste. Thumper. Games that reward the same kind of focused repetition that good coding rewards. I think there's something in that overlap worth exploring, so I do.

COME WATCH

I stream a few times a week, usually evenings Pacific time. Schedule lives on Twitch. VODs end up on YouTube with timestamps and notes. The blog posts here are the written companion — the stuff that's easier to read than to watch.

If you're building something cool and want to code together sometime, reach out. That's still the best part.
